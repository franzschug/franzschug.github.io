Urban Expansion Mapping Dubai

Project

This study aims to investigate the urban expansion of greater Dubai between 1990 - 2020.

Urbanization is one of the major attributes of the Anthropocene, with over half of the world’s population already living in cities and a projected further increase towards the end of the century (UN, 2018). Nearly 90 % of this urban growth is concentrated in less developed countries (Fenta et al. 2017). In spite of the positive sociological and economical effects, the process of urbanization strongly intervenes with climate and ecosystems on multiple scales, alternating existing atmospheric and hydrospheric properties often in a harmful way (Zhang et al. 2015, Myint and Okin 2009, Aslam et al. 2011). A lack of understanding and consideration for those negative consequences may lead to irreversible ecosystem deterioration (Alqurashi and Kumar 2014). Desert cities in particular have experienced strong growth, facilitated by socioeconomic development, improvements in regionally relevant technologies, such as desalination, and higher energy efficiency (Lazzarini et al. 2015). Despite their growing relevance and urbanization rate, regional urbanization studies in desert areas remain scarce. To tackle the lack of knowledge and planning identification of the distribution, cover and growth of impervious surfaces is crucial. With the opening of the Landsat archives multiple temporal remote sensing applications have arisen, enabling the exploitation of multi-decadal time series of satellite imagery (Schneider, 2012). Notwithstanding this major progress in data availability, remote-sensing based analysis of arid urban regions face manifold challenges. Apart from the ubiquitous trade-off between temporal coverage and the urban classification issue of mixed pixel signals given medium resolution sensors (Stefanov et al. 2001), arid urban areas additionally raise the issue of superimposed spectral signatures of bare soils, sparse density shrubs and impervious surface areas (Nassar et al. 2012). Given its geographic location and local climate conditions with virtually monthly cloud-free observations, Dubai is a prime example of a rapidly growing desert city to analyse. In this study, we utilise a random forest classification using spectral-temporal metrics based on Landsat data in half-decadal timesteps from 1990 to 2020. For the timesteps of 2015 and 2020 Landsat-based metrics and indices were supplemented with Sentinel-1 backscattering bands to address the question whether we can overcome the soil-impervious mixed spectral signatures. In addition, we want to analyse which metric has the biggest importance in arid-urban classifications and evaluate the classification algorithm utilising an area - adjusted accuracy assessment.

Methods

Satellite Data and Algorithm

To capture the urban expansion of Dubai and its surrounding area over time, we used annual collections of Landsat 5, Landsat 7, and Landsat 8 imagery in half-decadal timesteps from 1990 to 2020. For the timesteps of 2015 and 2020, we complemented Landsat imagery with re-projected and re-scaled Sentinel-1 C-band SAR backscatter information. We chose the random forest classification algorithm to predict the four land cover classes urban areas, water bodies, soil, and vegetation. All processing steps were performed within the Google Earth Engine, which enables high-performance data processing and gives access to all satellite archives and collections mentioned above (Gorelick et al. 2017).

Invariant Pixels and Training Data

Since training data selection in bygone years proves to be difficult, we based our training data selection on the invariant pixel signature generalization, as proposed by Gray and Song (2013). The purpose is to identify consistent spectral signatures with stable land cover within a training data collection of one baseline timestep, here 2020. We thereby calculated the City Block Distance (Manhattan Distance) based on the median band values of our baseline and a second timestep as summation of the absolute difference between timestep 1 and 2 within each band. As a result, we conversed our spectral information into a histogram and statistically filtered out points exceeding the 89th percentile, leaving only those pixels of assumed stable land cover.The training data collection for 2020 was selected based on Google Earth’s very high-resolution imagery with 100 points per strata.

Spectral-temporal metrics and Indices

In land cover classifications, calculating spectral-temporal metrics of dense time series satellite data is a widely used method, as they incorporate statistics that allow for a better discrimination of land use and land cover classes (Pflugmacher et al., 2019). Here, we used the standard deviation, 25th and 75th percentile and interval mean, as well as the median values over a year per band. Based on the median image over a year we further calculated the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), Normalized Burn Ratio (NBR), Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI), the three Tasseled Cap components brightness, greenness, wetness, as well as the Dry Bareness Index (DBSI) as proposed by Rasul et al. (2018). To these predictors we further added the mean band values of each year and for 2015 and 2020 we additionally supplemented the data with a Sentinel-1 C-band SAR composite of descending and ascending orbit parameter.

Discussion

Urban Expansion

We observed a continuous urban expansion and densification in the study period. The coastal core region grew and further infrastructure developed away from the city centre followed by suburbanization. The most intense period of urban expansion was the most recent period from 2015 to 2020. On average, the overall accuracy is 85.3% or 93.4% respectively. In total, urban extent increased from 590.3 km² (1990, adj. area) to 2074.3 km² (2020, adj. area), i.e. the proportion of urban extent trippled from 5% (1990) to 17.5% (2020).

Classification errors

The main challenge in this project was the discrimination of the urban and the soil class. Most errors arise from soil that is erroneously classified as urban area. This is mainly due to the dry and sandy soil characteristics in the study area. Extensive shadows of desert dunes constitute an additional source of misclassification. The confusion the two classes mainly occurs in 1990 and 1995 and decreases with time. The accuracy assesment strongly depends on the pixel selection in the stratified random sampling. Extending the sample size would increase the reliability of the results of the accuracy assessment.
